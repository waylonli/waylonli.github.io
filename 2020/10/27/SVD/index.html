Singular Value Decomposition
October 27, 2020 pm
631 words 12 mins

Last update：11 minutes ago

1. What is SVD (Singular Value Decomposition)?

1.1 Definition

Define $\bold{X} = (\vec{x_1})$ as a $n\times m$ matrix ($n \ge m$). A fraction of $\bold{X}$ is:
$$
\bold{X} = \bold{U}\bold{\Sigma}\bold{V}^\top
$$
where:

  • $\bold{U}$: $n \times n$ unitary matrix ($\bold{U}^* = \bold{U}^{-1}$)

  • $\bold{\Sigma}$: $n \times m$ rectangular diagonal matrix with non-negative real numbers on the diagonal

    $\bold{\Sigma} = \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_1 \\ & \sigma_2 \\ & & \ddots \\ & & & \sigma_m \\ 0 & 0 & \dots & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & \dots & 0 \end{bmatrix}$, $\sigma_1 \ge \sigma_2 \ge \dots \ge \sigma_m \ge 0$

  • $\bold{V}$: $m\times m$ unitary matrix

1.2 Singular value and singular vector

Singular value: the value on the diagonal in matrix $\bold{\Sigma}$

Singular vector:

  • Left singular vector: $\bold{U} = (\vec{u_1}, \vec{u_2}, \dots , \vec{u_n})$ are the left singular vectors of matrix $\bold{M}$
  • Right singular vector: $\bold{V} = (\vec{v_1}, \vec{v_2}, \dots , \vec{v_m})$ are the right singular vectors of matrix $\bold{M}$

2. Matrix Approximation

Notice that there are only $m$ singular values, we have the Eckand-Young theorem (1936):
$$
\bold{X} = \sigma_1 u_1 v_1^\top + \sigma_2 u_2 v_2^\top + \dots + \sigma_m u_m v_m^\top + \bold{0} \approx \bold{\tilde{U}}\bold{\tilde{\Sigma}}\bold{\tilde{V}}^\top
$$

3. Dominant Correlations

$$
\bold{X}^\top \bold{X} = \begin{bmatrix} x_1^\top x_1 & x_1^\top x_2 & \dots & x_1^\top x_m \\ x_2^\top x_1 & x_2^\top x_2 & \dots & x_2^\top x_m \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ x_m^\top x_1 & x_m^\top x_2 & \dots & x_m^\top x_m \end{bmatrix} \\ \ \\ \ \\ \bold{X} = \bold{\hat{U}}\bold{\hat{\Sigma}}\bold{V}^\top \text{(economy SVD)} \\ \ \\ \bold{X}^\top = \bold{V} \bold{\hat{\Sigma}}\bold{\hat{U}}^\top
\\ \ \\
\bold{X}^\top \bold{X} = \bold{V}\bold{\hat{\Sigma}}\underbrace{\bold{\hat{U}}^\top \bold{\hat{U}}}{\bold{\mathbb{I}}}\bold{\hat{\Sigma}}\bold{V}^\top = \bold{V} \hat{\bold{\Sigma}}^2 \bold{V}^\top \Rightarrow \bold{X}^\top \bold{X}\underbrace{\bold{V}}{\text{eigenvectors}} = \bold{V} \underbrace{\hat{\bold{\Sigma}}^2}_{\text{eigenvalues}} \\ \ \\

\bold{X} \bold{X}^\top = \bold{\hat{U}}\bold{\hat{\Sigma}}\underbrace{\bold{V}^\top \bold{V}}{\bold{\mathbb{I}}}\bold{\hat{\Sigma}}\bold{\hat{U}}^\top = \bold{\hat{U}} \hat{\bold{\Sigma}}^2 \bold{\hat{U}}^\top \Rightarrow \bold{X} \bold{X}^\top\underbrace{\bold{\hat{U}}}{\text{eigenvectors}} = \bold{\hat{U}} \underbrace{\hat{\bold{\Sigma}}^2}_{\text{eigenvalues}}
$$

4. SVD and Truncation

In $\bold{X} = \bold{U}\bold{\Sigma}\bold{V}^\top$, we can choose $r$ columns of $\bold{U}$ and $\bold{V}$. “How to choose $r$” is a fundamental problem in SVD.
$$
\bold{X} = \bold{\tilde{U}}_r\bold{\tilde{\Sigma}}_r\bold{\tilde{V}}_r^\top
$$
Generally we need to set a threshold for $\log \sigma$ and choose those $\sigma$s beyond the threshold on the diagonal of $\bold{\Sigma}$.

Recommending extra reading: Gavish-Donoho (2014)

Applying a Gaussian noise to $\bold{X}$:
$$
\bold{X} = \bold{X}{\text{true}} + \delta \bold{X}{\text{noise}}
$$

5. Example: Image Compression

To better understand what SVD does in a practical task, we consider a image processing task. It is easy to represent an image using a matrix. After calculate the SVD of this matrix, by choosing different $r$, we can keep different features of the image. When we choose to keep all the singular values, we can get the original image.
